The Crows


People who came to Roarke, California were always the ones searching for themselves in godforsaken places. Cath didn’t understand why people tried to find themselves in the middle of nowhere, but the ones that do always thought today was fifty years ago and cried a little when they saw a star-spangled banner chipping away on an old shed. Most travelers, though, walked in dreamers and out cynics. Here, the moon cast only rigid blue shadows, and you rarely walked upon stately, moss-draped ruins; the only history Cath came across were barns crawling with spiderwebs silhouetted like scars against the sky. They were the only wisps of life for miles.


Sometimes the crows came, though. Not just sometimes, actually. You saw hives on them roosting on elm trees, and when you couldn’t see them, you could hear their soft caws echoing through the woods. During the summer, newly nest-weaned fledglings stumbled on the roads, squawking toward the sky for parents that didn’t care for them anymore. No one in Roarke cared an ounce about them, either, other than uttering a grunt of gratitude when the birds ate their garbage. In fact, Cath hadn’t seen any fresh roadkill ever since she’d moved here because the crows would always reduce a corpse to a skeleton within an hour. You’d always know when and where something died because a flock would fly toward it and paint the sky black.


***


The world had turned into a labyrinth of mist and trees. Cath could see the criss-crossing crowns of elms against the moon, but nothing else. Her only thoughts were of home as she pedaled furiously through the rain.


A chirp, so quiet she almost missed it, sounded in front of her. The next second, she crested a rock, landing with a sickening crack and squeal. Cath braked, cursing. Of course the best time for her to get a flat tire was during a storm.


She looked beneath her bike and flinched. There was no flat tire. Under her wheel, bathed in a patch of blue moonlight, lay a baby crow, a nestling that the storm had blown from the trees. She had ridden her bike clean across its neck, and its mouth had sprung wide open from the pressure. Its last sounds--that piteous croak--ricocheted in her head.


Cath shuddered, reaching for the animal but pausing. Crows, or at least the crows here, were rumored to recognize who had wronged them and would seek retribution accordingly. The Smiths had had their whole house bombed with droppings after they’d removed a tree with three crows’ nests on it, and the Wangs had their screen doors pecked to oblivion. Perhaps if she covered the body, they wouldn’t come after her.


More so, the thought of another rider running the bird’s guts into the ground made her sick. Its eyes would bulge, and its brains would dribble out of its skull like egg yolk…


Cath rolled her bike off the corpse and nudged it toward the side of the road with her foot. Just like soccer practice. The bird proved an inadequate ball though. Its disproportionately heavy head flopped as if attached by noodles to its body.


Cath shuffled up some leaves to cover the body. A hasty burial, but thankfully, the dead didn’t have feelings.


Two mournful caws emanated from the trees behind her.


Just what she needed, to be hounded by the nestling’s parents. A small, scornful part of her screamed that she deserved it, running over a helpless creature and kicking it into a ditch like it was a soda can.


Something rustled in front of her. She couldn’t have distinguished anything from the dappled moonlight, but she thought she saw a flash of glassy eyes. Stillness.


Then, a crow stepped into the space between the shadows, its right eye cocked toward Cath.


Cath shrunk back before realizing it was just another crow trying to glimpse its offspring’s grave. Yet it did not hop, like crows so often did, toward its dead baby. It walked, its gait as fluid as that of a cat’s. Calculating.


Cath neither wanted to stay with the mourning crow or catch a cold in the rain. She jogged toward her bike, mounted it and pedaled hurriedly away. With a flap of its wings, the crow left as well.


***


Wind buffeted at Cath’s jacket, nearly blowing her into the side of the road. The storm seemed to have gathered even more strength in the past few minutes. Silvery sheets of rain and violent gusts rendered Cath nearly immobile, even as she cranked forward with all her might.


She considered taking shelter in the old shed up ahead. Perhaps her grandma could pick her up if she called for her, albeit begrudgingly.


The shed rose from the mist like a beacon, as if answering her prayers. It even seemed welcoming in the soft light of the moon, unlike during the daytime, when the sun exposed its cracked, rotten boards and half-chipped paint. Sometimes, Cath would see travelers gawk admirably at the American flag splashed on its side as if regarding some historic relic, then move on to the next peculiar scene. If they’d actually look inside, they would see rows upon rows of crows’ nests lining the trusses and wildflowers thriving under the constant supply of guano–a sickening sort of beauty.


Shivering, she ran her bike to the shed. The door was ajar like it always was. She hurried inside, sighing in relief as the soft pattering of rain on the roof replaced the sound of rain slapping against her face.


Suddenly, her foot slipped on something slimier than mud, and she fell face first onto the ground.


Even before her nose mashed into the ground, the smell of crow droppings wafted into Cath’s nose. Her cheek slipped on a puddle of the stuff, rattling her skull.


She groaned and hoisted herself up on an elbow. The fall had painted the underside of her body black and white with droppings, and with a jolt, she realized that she could not tell where her body started and ended; she had melted into the dappled ground in a grave of feces.


A crow was squatting in one of the nests along the upper beam, feathers ruffled against the cold. At the sound of Cath’s rising, it shook its feathers sleek and cocked its head towards her.


If she’d looked up, she would have seen more of them nesting above her, emerging one by one to enjoy this spectacle. Their bodies coated the roof until they eclipsed the crescent moon gaping through the cracks in the boards. Only then did she gaze upward to see what had blackened the sky. Through the sliver of moon that still shone from the door, Cath only saw glistening eyes and beaks twitching like insects. The birds hopped from beam to beam like small vultures, innocently inquisitive.


Heart racing, she valiantly tried to remove herself from the bird droppings, but they’d congealed into a scab around the underside of her body. When she lifted her arm, black and white ropes of slime snared it back to the ground. Worse, they’d siphoned away any feeling in her legs. She couldn’t even twitch them.


Cath pressed down the vomit that had been sloshing in her belly. Nothing in the world could have been this sticky and numbing, not even the spiderwebs that clung to every inch of Roarke. Something was terrible about these crows, from the way their eyes darted to the way they flocked to the way they looked upon their baby’s grave with sickening sorrow.


The only thing she could do was swim through it. She would swim through it all if it meant escaping the gaze of those birds. So she dug her fingers into the ground, through the feces and into the crumbled floorboards. Pulled herself toward the door. Dig. Hoist. Repeat.


Cath’s fingers met with grass after too long. Looking behind her, she saw a measly track her body had carved in the droppings. She breathed a sigh of relief when she spotted no crows behind her.


She felt something gently tugging on her leg. A crow was perched on the back of her calf, plucking at her thigh. It had worked through jeans and skin and was now feasting on her hamstring.


Diving its beak into the flesh, it surfaced with a squelch and a sliver of muscle in its beak. Bone glistened in the center of her leg.


Cath shrieked. The crow on her leg calmly raised its head. Behind it, like tombstones rising from the ground, four more crows’ heads emerged. They had been burying their beaks into her feet and had reduced both to tendon and bone.


“Get off me!” Cath screeched.


She tried raising her arm to swipe at the birds, but ropes of droppings clawed her back. With each futile attempt, she felt an insidious numbness bloom from each forearm until she had lost feeling in both.


Through the mop of hair that had congealed in front of her face, she could see the crow that had been eating her thigh in front of her. Cath screamed until she felt her eardrums burst and her throat fill with blood.


The crow cocked its head, stalking toward her until its beak nearly touched her nose. Up close, Cath observed that a crow’s feathers were quite soft, like meadow grass after rain.


They were the last thing she saw before the crow plucked out her eyes.

The Alleyway


During the storms of May, the Charles rages toward the ocean while lightning snaps Boston in two. Most people had already run for their Back Bay brownstones to warm tea and candlelight. Yet there are always some who are late. They sprint across the dark streets in soaked socks, rain tickling their tongues as they laugh at the sheer amount of water pouring from the sky.


I’d observe them from 23 as they fly through Public Alleyway 427 like nightingales. Content with staying dry but wistful for adventure, I’d instead imagine the world through their eyes. Narrow street. Silver sheets of rain. Rat. Squishy soles. No time to stop. Enough time to smile.


Through moments like these, I’ve come to realize the back alley is my favorite side of the house.


Well, there’s only two to choose from, and the front isn’t too shabby either. Outside 33, the Prudential rules its brownstone pawns alongside its deputy skyscrapers with a steely temper. Commonwealth Avenue’s trees, ripe green for the summer, sway gently to no breeze at all. Beneath them all, city people in their sparse urban streetwear sashay and jay walk and jog, leaving behind an echoing tune from their earbuds. They’re like birds of paradise to me, a Palo Altan indoctrinated into the cult of the Silicon Valley hoodie.


This side is beautiful, but it already knows that. Perhaps, then, I just favor the underdog. Yet I still replay stories from the alley in my head in a routine that might resemble love.


i.


Nearly every day, I bike to and from campus down the alley. I’m squinting, bleary eyed, in the morning sunlight when I leave, and the streetlamps cast their guiding lights towards tEp when I come home. No bustling traffic, just the crackle of my wheels on asphalt as I zoom under them. And when I go in the afternoon after a night of grinding on psets, the sun casts a glow around everything that is usually in shadow; I once saw an old man holding a bouquet of yellow flowers as he ambled past me, sporting a halo of sunlight and a smile. These daily, quiet moments from Boston and the bustling hall of the ‘tvte are some I cherish the most.


ii.


Most of my memories of the alley are made as an outside observer, however. From 23, I can see rats scurrying from dumpster to dumpster with dogs trying to break their leashes chasing them. There’s a different sort of ecosystem out there, one that runs on secretive trash trades and racoons’ conspiracies. This nightlife reminds me of my childhood make-believe stories, where Redwall-esque rodents fought wars with sewing needles under the full moon. A more imaginative time in my life perhaps, one I associate with carefree hours devoted just to think. Now, I ponder how the lives of these rats, moths, and even the flight of the feather under the moon are more complex than we can fathom. It fills me with a deep curiosity, knowing there is so much to be explored. Can we truly model that moth flitting in the light or the dust settling on that old mattress down to the molecule?


iii.


My favorite view of the back is from the roof because it’s no longer just the alley anymore. You can see the sprawling strip of Boston between tEp and the Charles, all chimneyed red-and-gray roofs. The next stratum is the Charles itself, then Cambridge in its quiet glory. I never fail to focus on the Big Dome first–-the blatant symbol of all the intellectual masochism and knowledge and hosedness that I’ve internalized in my two years here. It’s honed me into a cold, sharp blade, but is this what I want? Sometimes, all those late nights and seemingly unsolvable pset problems fill me with doubt; I think, I’m not built for this. I’m not built for inventions, or science, or anything that’s hard. I just want to close my eyes. But looking at that dome, I recall the triumphs I’ve made solving those unsolvable problems, submitting my research paper as the sun rose over East Campus or even just remembering how ripe the world is for discovering and letting that power me through everything else. Maybe the back side, and that certain Institute of Technology it houses, reflects that voice in the back of my mind telling me that things will work out in the end.

A Bildungsroman


The house is full of stories. I aged from newborn to near death in the span of a year. In a way then, the accounts below form a bildungsroman.


***


A carrot family was born from a single carrot and merged back into one again, albeit haphazardly. It still remains to be answered whether or not the carrot was more content as one being or separated into similar but disjoint entities, each with its own personality.


The vacuum cleaner was eating all the patterns on the floor, and I had to protect my family from them. But I knew that everyone was going to be safe because even the vacuum monsters are a carefully rehearsed act, of which I am but a player.


The creatures in the black light stairwell showed their fangs as well. Well, one of them was a blue mushroom so it only had its spores to show; it was near evening, so they were just starting to burst. Andy, Jess, and I were lucky to see them. It probably wasn’t a blue mushroom to them though.


***


In the kitchen.


Spices from all corners of the globe roared into me. Steam billowed from sinks ready to be ravished with the bowels of the meals to come. The house is a carnival, and its kitchen is equally puzzling and extroverted. It babbled into my ear like a boastful child until its laughter ricocheted painfully in my skull.


Antares and Mollie were cooking for the inhabitants of this strange world, frying, chop-chop-stirring away. So this is how the cogs of this house turn—through the manpower of its residents.


Jess had given me a mint chocolate sphere. It turned into goo in my mouth, then a sticky oobleck-y substance that fought its way down my throat. Being in that kitchen gave fuel to its struggle, prompting me to back away. Coughing, I crawled up to 23 to tell grand tales about my adventures in this strange land. The light show was buzzing, and Andy and Jess were almost melting away—the perfect time for a bedtime story. So I told them one.


I told them about the great columns of faces reaching into the blue abyss. The force of nature that vomited light onto us and cast shade for those who were worthy with its gnarled hands. The pug that went out of its mind trying to dig itself out of its own nose. All the while, the orange roses in my room had gone through three whole life cycles. I’m not sure if it made sense but it woke them up just in time for the festivities.


The “festivities” was dinner. A ritual, really. They ate and gathered in search of common ground amongst their disparate personalities, and they found lots of it, judging by the laughter. The steam from the fresh baked cornbread trilled up the stairs into me–the hearty, domestic smell of home.


Right then, I was watching a documentary of tEp. I felt like I knew what it truly was then from the view of a ghost, or at least bits of it that don’t quite make up the whole: domestic autumnal dinners with our squid overlords, aplomb with aprons and mitts and apples; a carnival of characters—the house members—breaking the 4th, 8th, and 32nd walls but not quite the last one as they acted out their carefully rehearsed play. We’re unique but still governed by expectations of social performance and puppeteered by forces unknown–hence the feeling of watching a documentary. It’s easy to study something once you’re removed from it, at least, but still hard to see the difference between what people are putting out as themselves versus what they truly are.


I’ve realized it’s the house, or less metaphorically, our history, that’s controlling us all to be these jumbled carnival actors. With its wisdom of all but 150 years, it’s seen enough to impose its will on lowly sprites like us. Is it the playwright, producer, and director then? It feels wrong but strangely comforting to be jangled like a keychain by some higher power made of earth and wood. It’s creating order from chaos: an ill-splattered blot of paint was always meant to be in this endless fractal, and the little pores in the ceiling tiles are convalescing into some sort of underlying pattern. But what if it’s pointless to try to create order from these things? It should just let a paint splatter be a paint splatter, and not have to impose my will on it. Rather cruel of it to do so, actually. And despite all its scheming, this brownstone mansion has yet to fully grasp the reins over its residents. I see it in our covert midnight conversations, the warm puddles of people that form periodically on the couch, the occasional rooftop glimpse across the river. There’s always more than a house can capture.


***


This museum or carnival representation perhaps arises from my laziness in forming a narrative of my experiences, carelessly anthologizing them into disparate but vaguely connected events. I don’t think I’ll weave the threads between all these stories, but they are fun to think about on their own. Or pretending everything is part of a carnival is me refusing to deal with reality.


Speaking of which, it’s almost time to leave this place. Well, I still have two years left, but time runs faster here. It just struck me how ephemeral and unique a story at tEp can be. Our customs are but a guideline to how we shape this corner of the world, meant to inspire rather than reign. Each actor passing through its double doors, myself included, exits having created a new piece of our culture to be remembered until future ones overwrite it. I wonder, what will my contribution be? Is it my late-night footsteps, my laugh, or even just these jumbled ramblings? When I step out the doors one last time, I hope I’ll have an answer.

The Quantum Monster


i. Primordial orange juice


In the beginning, there was nothing. A few seconds later, there I was. I did not emerge from a womb, for there were no wombs, nor was I a slimy, amniotic newborn, for that would look gross.


I was simply the Quantum Monster, a speck of pulp spawned from the sunset-colored void.


For millennia, I waded amongst the nebulas and neutron stars of the newborn universe, growing larger but never older. I wondered if there were others like me, for I was lonely, and the stars did not speak.


All this tumbling through space gave me time to think, so my brain grew as well, and soon, I became more well-minded than I was well-muscled.


I concluded the greatest mystery in the universe was size. I knew I was made up of protons and neutrons, mushed together to form atoms that formed the stardust that was me, bigger still. And beyond me swam my nest of galaxies, vast to me yet miniscule compared to the frontiers around. Beyond those, and inside the particles that made me? I’ll never know.


Size. It defeated me.


Thinking about this lazily drew my attention to my unchecked augmentation. With a jolt, I realized I was clutching a planet. I stared at the pale orb, marveling at its electron-like moons.


It was shrinking.


I frowned as it shrank before my eyes to the size of my nostril. I realized that it wasn’t shrinking; I was growing. I contemplated how I’d grow and grow until I’d lie on the galaxies like a child in a bygone crib, toes pressed against the edge of the universe. Would I rip her open, or will she hold her own, boxing me in until my spine crumpled between her walls?


Size. It defeats all.


I did not have high hopes for myself or the universe. I’d spent my whole life waiting for another of my kind, but the universe did not deliver one. To her, I was but a speck of stardust. Unworthy. She had bigger matters, anti, dark, and whatever other types there were, to deal with.


So I reclined on an asteroid belt, juggling the now gnat-sized planet between my hands, content with my fate.


ii. Dimensioned-ness


On the twenty-second billionth day of my sojourn in this dimension, I finally encountered the edge of my territory. I was napping on the Muskrat Cluster when a tingling sensation in my pseudopodia awakened me. The galaxies around my extremities had started to give way to a simmering sarsaparilla fog.


I looked closely at the fog, trying to discern any hardy signs of sentience, and found none. I was numb to loneliness anyway. Entropy teasing me with its sadistically intriguing phenomena gave me no real ideas on how to escape this cage. That is, until a tinny voice rasped from the depths of the fog.


“Come closer, under-dimensioned child,” it said.


This was at last surprising. A mind like mine, or close to it--cradling galaxies in my arms and diving into black holes was nothing compared to this. I heeded its order, naively assuming it held the same curiosity as I did.


A curved, wicked, and tiny tooth came into view. It spoke again.


“A bit chubby. I will see what I can do. Give me your appendage.”


“Who are you?”


“I am the Boy. Give me your appendage. Preferably chilled.”


I gave the Boy a cold one, tightening some of the flesh. It was a joyous feeling to be judged at last, yet “chubby” was an unexpected identifier. Did that mean there were enough Quantum Monsters to warrant a weight distribution? Did I represent a larger percentage of them by mass and volume, and thus in territorial distribution?


The Boy cocked its tooth to the side, examining the flesh before it. Without warning, it sliced into me with that wicked thing with a loud lick, drawing forth an explosion of my circulatory marbles.


The click echoed ominously in space, even though there was no medium to carry it through. I expected something more climactic.


And it came.


A second click, millions of decibels louder than the first, boomed through space time. My own scream was lost in the storm. For half a moment, I only saw a whirl of colors engulfing me, like the light from all the supernovas that ever happened. More beautiful than anything I’d ever seen.


All good things had to come to an end. A white wave washed away the colors, and for a few seconds, I feared it had washed away my sight too until the blackness of space swam before me. Stars, seemingly further away than before, glinted blurrily.


I turned my head to the right and saw the Boy’s smeared visage. The tooth turned out to be one of many, lined by full lips. Its forehead extended past its spine, so long that it sat on it cross-legged. I held my hand out in front of me. Its face was bigger than it.


Beyond it loomed a pale blue planet, dotted with moons--the same one I’d been juggling between my hands all those years ago.


A calloused hand slapped me square on the cheek, jolting me out of my reverie.


“I could shout at you some more, but I don’t feel like it,” said The Jim. “I’ve never lost my voice after all this time.”


“You were not shouting. But thank you.” I said.
